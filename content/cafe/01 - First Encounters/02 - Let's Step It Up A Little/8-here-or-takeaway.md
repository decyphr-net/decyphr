# Here Or Takeaway

Now that you've gotten through the full conversation, let's take a look at some ways that this conversation could naturally be expanded by.

A staple in any conversation like this is "for here or takeway?"

Let's take a look at where in the conversation this might occur:
- **Barista**
> Dia duit! Cad is féidir liom a fháil duit?
> DEE-ah gwich! Cod iss FEH-dir lum uh ayl gwich?
> Hello! What can I get for you?

- **Customer**
> Dia is Muire duit. Caifé latte agus tae glas, le do thoil.
> DEE-ah iss MWIR-eh gwich. Kaff-AY LAH-teh ah-gus teh gloss, leh duh hull.
> Hi, a latte and a green tea, please.

- **Barista**
> Cén bainne atá uait sa latte?
> Kayn BAN-yuh ah-TAW oo-it suh LAH-teh?
> Which milk would you like in the latte?

- **Customer**
> Bainne coirce, le do thoil.
> BAN-yuh KUR-keh, leh duh hull.
> Oat milk, please.

- **Barista**
> Anseo nó le tabhairt leat?
> An-SHO noh leh TOW-urt lyat?
> For here or takeaway?

`Anseo` means "here", and `nó` means "or". `Le tabhairt leat` literally means “to take with you.” From the barista’s point of view, they’re asking if you want to take it with you.

Let's take a look at the more straight-forward response to this question first:

- **Customer**
> Anseo, le do thoil.
> An-SHO, leh duh hull.
> For here, please.

Simple and direct.

Now, if we wanted it for takeaway, we'd simply say:
- **Customer**
> Le tabhairt liom, le do thoil
> Leh TOW-urt lum, leh duh hull
> Takeaway, please

You’ll notice it changes slightly from `leat` to `liom`. That’s just because you’re now speaking from your own point of view - "with me" instead of "with you."
